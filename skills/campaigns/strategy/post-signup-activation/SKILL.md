# Post-Signup Activation

## Overview

Design and optimize what happens after someone takes any action in the the marketing funnel — quiz completion, webinar registration, program enrollment, or retreat booking. The confirmation experience IS the first taste of the transformation container. Every post-action touchpoint either builds trust and momentum or leaks it.

## Core Principles

### 1. Time-to-Felt-Experience

In healing and education, value is felt, not measured. The question is not "how fast can they use the product" but "how quickly does someone feel something shift?" Every post-signup moment should deliver a felt experience: a moment of recognition, insight, relief, or belonging.

- Remove every barrier between the action they just took and feeling something meaningful
- Can they feel the quality of the container before they pay? (Pre-purchase felt experience)
- The speed of the first felt moment predicts whether they take the next step

### 2. One Goal Per Interaction

- Do not overwhelm with logistics, options, and next steps simultaneously
- Each touchpoint has ONE purpose: deliver a feeling, convey a single piece of information, or invite a single action
- Save secondary information for follow-up messages

### 3. Progress Creates Motivation

- Show people where they are on their journey
- Acknowledge each step as meaningful (not just transactional)
- Make the path ahead visible without making it feel heavy

### 4. Confirmation Quality Signals Paid Experience Quality

- If the free experience feels generic, they assume the paid experience will too
- If the free experience feels personal, intentional, and warm, they trust the container
- Every confirmation email, SMS, and page is an audition for the paid experience

## Activation Moments by Funnel Stage

### Post-Quiz

**What just happened:** Someone completed an assessment, quiz, or intake form. They shared something personal. Honor that.

1. **Result delivery** — "Here is what this means for you"
   - Frame results as insight, not diagnosis
   - Use language that normalizes their experience: "Many people in your situation feel..."
   - Personalize the narrative based on their answers — do not deliver a generic result page
   - The result itself is the felt experience — make it land

2. **Personalized next-step invitation**
   - Based on their quiz segment, recommend ONE specific next action
   - Frame the invitation as a natural continuation, not a sales pitch
   - Example: "Based on what you shared, this upcoming session was designed for exactly where you are"

3. **Segment-based follow-up**
   - Tag the person by quiz result segment in Attio
   - Trigger the appropriate follow-up sequence (email, SMS) based on segment
   - Follow-up content should reference their specific result, not generic messaging

### Post-Webinar-Registration

**What just happened:** Someone said "I want to learn more." The confirmation must build anticipation, not just confirm logistics.

1. **Confirmation that builds anticipation**
   - Lead with what they will experience, not logistics
   - Bad: "You're registered! Here are the details."
   - Good: "Something is about to shift. Here is what we will explore together..."
   - Include a compelling teaser: a question to sit with, a short testimonial, or a preview of the transformation

2. **Show-up optimization**
   - Calendar add link (Google Calendar, Apple Calendar, Outlook) — in confirmation email AND SMS
   - "What to expect" content: format, duration, what to have ready, how to prepare mentally
   - Social proof: "X people are joining" or "people from Y backgrounds are attending"
   - Reminders: 24 hours before (email), 1 hour before (SMS), 5 minutes before (SMS with join link)

3. **Pre-webinar nurture sequence**
   - Day of registration: confirmation + anticipation builder
   - 2-3 days before: "Something to think about before we meet" — a reflection question or short content piece
   - Day before: logistics reminder + social proof
   - Do NOT send more than 3-4 emails total between registration and webinar

### Post-Enrollment

**What just happened:** Someone committed. They may feel excited, nervous, vulnerable, or all three. Meet them where they are.

1. **Welcome message** — warmth first, logistics second
   - Lead with: "Welcome. We are glad you are here."
   - Acknowledge the courage it takes to commit to this kind of work
   - Do NOT lead with a wall of logistics, login instructions, or task lists

2. **Program orientation**
   - After the warm welcome (separate message, 24h later), provide a clear but gentle orientation
   - Checklist of 3-5 items, ordered by felt-experience first:
     - First item should deliver an insight or connection (e.g., "Watch this 3-minute welcome video from [facilitator]")
     - Middle items: practical setup (community access, schedule overview)
     - Last item: optional but meaningful (e.g., "Set an intention for your journey")
   - Progress indicator visible but not pressuring

3. **First module or quick win**
   - Within 48 hours, give them access to ONE thing they can complete in under 10 minutes
   - This is not about "getting ahead" — it is about feeling like they have started
   - The first action should feel rewarding, not like homework

4. **Community introduction**
   - If there is a cohort or community space, facilitate a warm introduction
   - Provide a simple prompt: "Share one word for how you are feeling right now"
   - Lower the barrier — do not ask for long introductions

5. **"You are not behind" permission email**
   - Send 5-7 days after enrollment
   - People in healing contexts carry perfectionism and shame about "doing it right"
   - Explicitly say: "There is no behind. You are exactly where you need to be."
   - Normalize non-linear engagement
   - This email consistently gets the highest open and reply rates in healing/education programs

### Post-Retreat-Booking

**What just happened:** Someone committed significant money and time to an in-person experience. The stakes feel high. The preparation period IS part of the container.

1. **Preparation sequence** (begin 4-6 weeks before retreat)
   - Week 1: Welcome + "what to expect" overview (format, schedule shape, facilitator introductions)
   - Week 2-3: Practical logistics (what to bring, travel details, dietary needs form)
   - Week 3-4: Mental/physical preparation guidance (meditation practice, journaling prompts, dietary recommendations if applicable)
   - Week 4-5: Intention-setting exercise ("What are you hoping to release? What are you hoping to invite in?")
   - Week 5-6: Pre-arrival cohort connection (introduce others in the group, with consent)

2. **Logistics delivery**
   - Separate logistics from emotional preparation — do not mix them in the same email
   - Logistics emails should be clear, scannable, and complete
   - Include: arrival time, address/directions, parking, what to bring, what NOT to bring, emergency contact

3. **Pre-arrival cohort connection**
   - With consent, share first names and one sentence from each person's intention
   - This reduces arrival anxiety ("Will I belong here?") and builds anticipation
   - Optional: create a shared space (group chat, forum) for pre-arrival connection

4. **Intention-setting exercise**
   - Send as a standalone email 1-2 weeks before
   - Simple prompt, not a lengthy worksheet
   - Frame as invitation, not assignment: "If you feel called, spend a few minutes with this question..."

## Checklist Pattern

When using a checklist for any funnel stage:

- **3-7 items** — fewer than 3 feels trivial, more than 7 feels overwhelming
- **Order by felt-experience first** — the first item should deliver insight, connection, or warmth. Logistics come after.
- **Progress indicator visible** — show completion without making it feel like a test
- **Each item includes:**
  - Clear action (verb-first)
  - What they will get from it (benefit, not feature)
  - Estimated time ("2 minutes")
- **Never block access** behind checklist completion — it is a guide, not a gate
- **Celebrate completion** with acknowledgment, not confetti: "You are all set. We will see you soon."

## Multi-Channel Coordination

### Email vs. SMS — Different Jobs

| Channel | Use for | Tone |
|---|---|---|
| Email | Content, stories, preparation exercises, detailed logistics | Warm, spacious, reflective |
| SMS | Reminders, time-sensitive logistics, join links, check-ins | Brief, warm, direct |

### Timing Rules

- Never send email and SMS with the same content on the same day
- SMS is for moments, not narratives — keep under 160 characters when possible
- Email carries the story; SMS carries the nudge
- Reminder cadence: email at 24h before, SMS at 1h and 5min before
- Post-event follow-up: email within 2 hours (while the feeling is fresh)

## Stalled User Detection and Re-Engagement

### Registered But Did Not Attend Webinar

- Wait 24 hours, then send warmth-based re-engagement (not guilt)
- "We missed you — and we recorded it for you. Watch the replay whenever feels right."
- Include replay link with no expiration pressure (or if there is expiration, state it honestly)
- Never: "You missed out!" or "Don't miss the next one!"
- One follow-up is enough. Two maximum. Do not chase.

### Enrolled But Did Not Start Program

- Wait 5-7 days before re-engagement
- Lead with: "You are not behind. There is no wrong time to begin."
- Lower the barrier: suggest ONE tiny action ("Watch this 2-minute welcome video")
- Offer support: "If something is getting in the way, reply to this email. We are here."
- Do not list everything they are "missing" — that increases shame, not motivation

### Booked Retreat But Went Quiet

- Wait 2 weeks of no engagement before reaching out
- Check in with genuine care: "We wanted to check in. How are you feeling about the upcoming retreat?"
- Offer to address concerns: "If anything has come up — logistics, nerves, questions — we are here."
- No pressure, no urgency, no "spots are filling up"
- If they need to cancel or reschedule, make it easy and graceful

### Universal Re-Engagement Principles

- Warmth, not urgency. Never guilt. Never FOMO.
- One clear, low-barrier action per re-engagement message
- Always offer a human connection option ("reply to this email," "book a call")
- Maximum 2 re-engagement attempts per stall. After that, respect their silence.

## Engagement Loop

**Structure:** Trigger -> Action -> Reward -> Investment

In this organization's context, this is NOT gamification. No badges, points, streaks, or leaderboards.

- **Trigger**: An email, SMS, or community notification that sparks curiosity or recognition
- **Action**: Watch a video, read a reflection, join a live session, complete a journaling prompt
- **Reward**: Insight, felt shift, sense of connection, recognition of their own growth
- **Investment**: Share a reflection with the community, set an intention, invite a friend, deepen their practice

The loop is powered by meaning, not mechanics. Each cycle should leave the person feeling more connected to their own process, not more dependent on the platform.

## Funnel Analysis Methodology

### Where Are People Stalling?

Map the drop-off between every funnel stage:

```
Quiz completion -> Webinar registration -> Webinar attendance -> Enrollment -> Program start -> Retreat booking
     100%              X%                      Y%                  Z%            W%               V%
```

1. Identify the largest percentage drop between any two stages
2. Focus activation optimization on that transition first
3. For each drop-off point, ask:
   - Is the next step clear?
   - Is the gap between action and felt-experience too long?
   - Is the ask too big? (Can it be broken into smaller steps?)
   - Is there enough social proof at this transition?
   - Is the messaging warm enough, or does it feel transactional?

### Metrics to Track

- **Show-up rate**: % of registrants who attend (webinars, sessions, retreats)
- **Time-to-first-engagement**: How quickly after signup does someone take the next action?
- **Sequence completion rate**: % who receive and engage with each message in a sequence
- **Re-engagement recovery rate**: % of stalled users who re-engage after outreach
- **Stage progression rate**: % who move from one funnel stage to the next

## Experiment Ideas

### Flow Simplification
- Reduce confirmation page to ONE next action instead of multiple options
- Test immediate calendar-add prompt vs. including it only in confirmation email
- Test video welcome vs. text welcome in confirmation

### Personalization
- Segment confirmation content by quiz result or registration source
- Test personalized subject lines referencing their specific situation
- A/B test "from a person" vs. "from [Organization name]" sender name

### Quick Wins
- Test sending a 2-minute audio reflection immediately after registration
- Test including a journaling prompt in confirmation vs. sending it separately
- Test "what to expect" content in confirmation vs. as a separate pre-event email

### Email and SMS Sequencing
- Test 2-email pre-webinar sequence vs. 3-email sequence (measure show-up rate)
- Test SMS reminder at 1 hour vs. 30 minutes before event
- Test "who else is coming" social proof email vs. no social proof

## Steps

1. **Identify the funnel stage** — which post-action moment is being optimized? (post-quiz, post-registration, post-enrollment, post-booking)
2. **Audit the current experience** — what happens right now after someone takes this action? Map every touchpoint: confirmation page, emails, SMS, silence gaps.
3. **Identify the felt-experience gap** — where is the longest gap between action and feeling something meaningful? That is the priority fix.
4. **Design the activation sequence** — using the stage-specific guidance above, draft the touchpoint sequence with exact timing, channel, and content purpose for each message.
5. **Apply the checklist pattern** if the stage involves multiple setup or preparation steps (3-7 items, felt-experience first).
6. **Define stalled-user criteria and re-engagement** — what counts as "stalled" for this stage? Design the warmth-based re-engagement sequence (max 2 attempts).
7. **Set up measurement** — define the metrics for this stage and establish baselines before making changes.
8. **Propose 2-3 experiments** — specific A/B tests or flow changes to validate, with clear success metrics.

## Output

Deliver as a structured plan:

- **Stage**: Which funnel moment
- **Current state**: What happens now (if audited)
- **Activation sequence**: Numbered touchpoints with timing, channel, content purpose, and felt-experience goal
- **Checklist** (if applicable): 3-7 items with action, benefit, and estimated time
- **Stalled user plan**: Detection criteria + re-engagement sequence
- **Metrics**: What to measure and baseline targets
- **Experiments**: 2-3 testable changes with success criteria
