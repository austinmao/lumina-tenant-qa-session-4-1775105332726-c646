# Marketing Psychology & Mental Models

## Overview

Use this skill for any conversion-focused marketing task: landing pages, email
sequences, funnel architecture, webinar scripts, ad copy, or pricing displays.
It provides 70+ mental models organized by category, each with an application
lens for education, experience, and transformation businesses. Every model
includes a the organization ethics check.

## Steps

1. **Identify the conversion goal** -- What specific action should the reader take?
   (register, apply, enroll, attend, share, refer)
2. **Select 2-3 relevant models** from the categories below that match the goal
   and the reader's current stage (awareness, consideration, decision)
3. **Apply models to the copy or page** -- weave the principles into headlines,
   body, CTAs, layout, and sequencing
4. **Check the Ethics Layer** (see section below) -- confirm every
   application passes the "use freely / use with care / never use" filter
5. **Check voice-calibration** -- all copy must also pass the `voice-calibration`
   skill's E1 rule: the reader is on their journey, not broken

## Output

When applying this skill, produce:
- The specific models selected and why they fit this context
- Concrete copy or structural recommendations citing the model by name
- Ethics layer verdict for each model used (freely / with care)
- Any voice-calibration flags

## 1. Foundational Thinking Models

Strategy-level models that sharpen what to work on before writing a word.

| Model | Definition | Application |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **First Principles** | Decompose problems to base truths; rebuild from there | Before launching a retreat campaign, ask "why do people seek this experience?" rather than copying competitor funnels |
| **Jobs to Be Done** | People hire solutions for outcomes, not features | Attendees do not buy a retreat -- they hire an experience to process grief, find clarity, or reconnect with purpose |
| **Circle of Competence** | Operate where you have genuine expertise | the organization owns deep credentialed authority in guided psychedelic healing -- lead with that, not generic wellness claims |
| **Inversion** | Ask "what would guarantee failure?" then prevent each item | List everything that would kill enrollment -- confusing eligibility, no trust signals, unclear safety protocols -- and address each |
| **Occam's Razor** | Simplest explanation is usually correct | If webinar registrations dropped, check the form and page speed before rebuilding the entire funnel |
| **Pareto Principle (80/20)** | ~80% of results come from ~20% of efforts | Identify which 20% of touchpoints (e.g., the webinar replay email) drive most enrollments and invest there |
| **Local vs. Global Optima** | Best nearby solution may not be the best overall | Optimizing email subject lines is local; the global question is whether email is the right channel for this audience segment |
| **Theory of Constraints** | Every system has one bottleneck limiting throughput | If the funnel converts well but traffic is low, more CRO will not help -- fix the traffic constraint first |
| **Opportunity Cost** | Every choice forecloses alternatives | Time spent on a low-ROI channel is time not spent deepening the channel that already works |
| **Law of Diminishing Returns** | Additional investment yields progressively smaller gains | The tenth testimonial on a page adds less trust than the first three |
| **Second-Order Thinking** | Consider effects of effects | A discount code boosts enrollment (first order) but may train prospects to wait for promotions (second order) |
| **Map is Not Territory** | Models represent reality but are not reality | A persona document is useful; real participants are more complex -- validate assumptions with actual conversations |
| **Probabilistic Thinking** | Estimate likelihoods, plan for multiple outcomes | Do not stake the quarter on one campaign -- spread across channels and plan for underperformance scenarios |
| **Barbell Strategy** | Combine extreme safety with small high-risk bets | Put 80% of budget into proven channels; allocate 20% to experimental formats or audiences |

## 2. Buyer Psychology

Models that explain how prospective participants think, decide, and behave.

| Model | Definition | Application |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **Fundamental Attribution Error** | Attributing others' behavior to character, not circumstance | When a prospect does not enroll, examine the process (confusing application, unclear pricing) before assuming lack of commitment |
| **Mere Exposure Effect** | Familiarity breeds preference | Consistent brand presence across email, social, podcast, and community builds trust before a prospect ever visits the enrollment page |
| **Availability Heuristic** | People judge likelihood by how easily examples come to mind | Vivid participant stories make transformation feel achievable -- make positive outcomes easy to imagine |
| **Confirmation Bias** | People seek information confirming existing beliefs | Understand what your audience already believes about psychedelic healing and align messaging with those beliefs before expanding their frame |
| **Lindy Effect** | The longer something has survived, the longer it will continue | Proven principles (clear value, social proof, trust signals) outlast trendy growth hacks |
| **Mimetic Desire** | People want things because others want them | Show that respected, thoughtful people choose this experience -- community belonging triggers desire |
| **Sunk Cost Fallacy** | Continuing because of past investment, not future value | Recognize when to retire an underperforming campaign -- past spend does not justify future spend |
| **Endowment Effect** | People value things more once they feel ownership | Free workshops, guided meditations, or preparation materials let prospects feel connected before they enroll |
| **IKEA Effect** | People value what they helped create | Interactive assessments, self-reflection exercises, or intake questionnaires increase investment and perceived value |
| **Zero-Price Effect** | Free is psychologically different from any price | A free offer (workshop, guide, webinar) triggers irrational preference -- the jump from any cost to zero is disproportionately powerful |
| **Hyperbolic Discounting** | Strong preference for immediate over future rewards | Emphasize what begins immediately ("Start your preparation journey today") rather than distant outcomes alone |
| **Status-Quo Bias** | People prefer the current state | Reduce friction to change: clear next steps, simple applications, "here is exactly what happens when you apply" |
| **Default Effect** | People accept pre-selected options | Pre-select the recommended program tier or retreat date to guide the decision |
| **Paradox of Choice** | Too many options overwhelm and paralyze | Limit program options on the enrollment page -- two or three pathways, not seven |
| **Goal-Gradient Effect** | People accelerate effort as they approach a goal | Show application progress ("Step 2 of 3") and preparation milestones to drive completion |
| **Peak-End Rule** | Experiences judged by the peak moment and the ending | Design a memorable peak in the webinar (a powerful story or insight) and a strong close (clear next step, warmth) |
| **Zeigarnik Effect** | Unfinished tasks occupy the mind | "You have started your application -- complete the last step" creates pull to finish |
| **Pratfall Effect** | A small flaw makes competent entities more relatable | Honest acknowledgment ("This work is not for everyone") increases trust and differentiation |
| **Curse of Knowledge** | Experts forget what it is like not to know | The team understands ceremony deeply; newcomers do not -- test all copy with people unfamiliar with the space |
| **Mental Accounting** | People treat money differently based on framing | "$83 per day of your retreat" reframes a multi-thousand-dollar investment into a daily experience cost |
| **Regret Aversion** | People avoid actions that might cause regret | Money-back policies, transparent refund terms, and "no-pressure" messaging reduce the fear of a wrong decision |
| **Bandwagon Effect / Social Proof** | People follow what others are doing | Participant counts, testimonials, alumni community size, and "featured in" logos create confidence |

## 3. Persuasion Principles

Models for ethically influencing enrollment and engagement decisions.

| Model | Definition | Application |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **Reciprocity** | People feel obligated to return value received | Free content (preparation guides, meditation recordings, educational webinars) creates reciprocal goodwill before any ask |
| **Commitment & Consistency** | People stay consistent with prior commitments | A small first step (newsletter signup, free workshop) makes the next step (application) psychologically consistent |
| **Authority Bias** | People defer to credentialed experts | Feature clinical credentials, published research, facilitator training hours, and institutional partnerships |
| **Liking / Similarity Bias** | People say yes to those they relate to | Use alumni voices that mirror the prospect's identity -- "I was a skeptic too" from someone like them |
| **Unity Principle** | Shared identity drives influence | Position the community as a group the prospect already belongs to: seekers, healers, people in transition |
| **Scarcity** | Limited availability increases perceived value | Communicate real seat limits and actual enrollment windows -- never fabricate scarcity (see Ethics Layer) |
| **Foot-in-the-Door** | Small compliance leads to larger compliance | Free offer leads to webinar, webinar leads to application, application leads to enrollment -- each step builds on the last |
| **Door-in-the-Face** | Large request first makes the real request seem reasonable | Present the premium immersive program first; the standard retreat feels accessible by contrast |
| **Loss Aversion** | Losses feel roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains | Frame ethically: "what becomes possible when you take this step" -- not "what you will lose by waiting" (see Ethics Layer) |
| **Anchoring** | The first number seen shapes all subsequent judgment | Show the value of the full experience first (preparation + ceremony + integration = months of support) before revealing the investment |
| **Decoy Effect** | A third inferior option makes the target option look better | A limited-features tier makes the recommended program look like the clear choice |
| **Framing Effect** | Presentation changes perception of identical information | "94% of participants report lasting positive change" outperforms "6% report no change" -- same data, different impact |
| **Contrast Effect** | Things are judged relative to what they appear beside | Show the before state (stuck, searching, disconnected) clearly so the after (clarity, connection, purpose) feels vivid |

## 4. Pricing Psychology

Models for how people perceive and respond to investment framing.

| Model | Definition | Application |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **Charm Pricing / Left-Digit Effect** | $X,997 feels significantly less than $X,000 | Use for value-positioned programs; avoid for premium offerings where round numbers signal quality |
| **Rounded-Price (Fluency) Effect** | Round numbers signal premium quality | Use round numbers ($5,000) for premium retreat experiences -- they process as "quality," not "deal" |
| **Rule of 100** | Under $100: percentage discounts feel larger; over $100: absolute discounts feel larger | For a $3,000 program, "$500 early enrollment savings" outperforms "17% off" |
| **Price Relativity / Good-Better-Best** | People judge prices relative to presented options | Three tiers where the middle is the target -- the premium tier makes it look reasonable, the basic tier provides an anchor |
| **Mental Accounting (Pricing)** | Same price framed differently changes perception | "$167 per month" or "$40 per day of retreat" reframes a lump sum into digestible units tied to the experience |

## 5. Design & Delivery Psychology

Models for structuring pages, sequences, and user experiences.

| Model | Definition | Application |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **Hick's Law** | More choices = slower decisions = more abandonment | One clear CTA per page section; fewer form fields; one recommended program path |
| **AIDA Funnel** | Attention, Interest, Desire, Action | Structure every landing page and email to move through each stage in order -- capture attention before building desire |
| **Rule of 7** | Prospects need ~7 touchpoints before converting | Build multi-touch sequences across email, social, podcast, and community -- a single touchpoint rarely converts for high-investment decisions |
| **Nudge Theory / Choice Architecture** | Small presentation changes significantly influence decisions | Strategic defaults, deliberate ordering of options, and friction reduction guide behavior without restricting choice |
| **BJ Fogg Behavior Model** | Behavior = Motivation x Ability x Prompt | All three must be present: high motivation + easy process + clear prompt at the right moment |
| **EAST Framework** | Make desired behaviors Easy, Attractive, Social, Timely | Reduce application friction (easy), make the opportunity compelling (attractive), show alumni doing it (social), present at the right moment (timely) |
| **COM-B Model** | Behavior requires Capability, Opportunity, Motivation | Can they afford it and attend? (capability) Is the path to enrollment clear? (opportunity) Do they feel called? (motivation) |
| **Activation Energy** | Initial energy required to start prevents action | Make the first step trivially easy -- "Watch a 3-minute video" not "Fill out this 20-field application" |
| **North Star Metric** | One metric capturing the core value delivered | Identify the metric (qualified applications, retreat completions, alumni referrals) and align all marketing toward it |
| **Cobra Effect** | Incentives that backfire and produce the opposite result | A referral bonus might attract people gaming the system rather than genuine community referrals -- test incentive structures carefully |

## 6. Growth & Compounding Models

Models for how marketing efforts compound and scale over time.

| Model | Definition | Application |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **Feedback Loops** | Output becomes input, creating cycles | More alumni stories lead to more trust, which leads to more enrollments, which leads to more alumni stories |
| **Compounding** | Small consistent gains accumulate into large results | Consistent content, SEO, and community building compound -- early investment pays exponential returns over years |
| **Network Effects** | Value increases as more people participate | An alumni community becomes more valuable with each cohort -- new members get access to a richer network |
| **Flywheel Effect** | Sustained effort creates self-maintaining momentum | Content attracts prospects, who become participants, who create testimonials, which attract more prospects |
| **Switching Costs** | The cost of changing to an alternative | Deep preparation journeys, community bonds, and facilitator relationships create natural retention without manipulation |
| **Exploration vs. Exploitation** | Balance trying new things with optimizing what works | Allocate most resources to proven channels; reserve a portion for experimental formats and audiences |
| **Critical Mass / Tipping Point** | Threshold after which growth becomes self-sustaining | Focus on reaching critical mass in one audience segment (e.g., therapists, veterans) before expanding broadly |
| **Survivorship Bias** | Focusing on visible successes while ignoring invisible failures | Study why past campaigns failed, not just why the successful ones worked -- the hit you admire had many invisible failures behind it |

## the organization Ethics Layer

**This section is mandatory for every application of the models above.**

All marketing psychology must be filtered through these three tiers before use.

### Use Freely

These principles align with honest, respectful communication:

- **Social proof** -- real participant stories, real numbers
- **Authority** -- genuine credentials, published research, verifiable partnerships
- **Reciprocity** -- give real value (workshops, guides, meditations) before asking
- **Commitment & Consistency** -- small authentic steps leading to larger ones
- **Anchoring** -- honest price context and value framing
- **Framing effects** -- presenting true information in the most resonant way
- **Story transportation** -- genuine transformation narratives from real participants
- **Mere exposure** -- consistent brand presence across channels
- **IKEA effect** -- self-reflection exercises and interactive preparation that build genuine investment

### Use With Care

These principles require strict conditions:

- **Scarcity** -- ONLY when real: actual seat limits set by safety protocols, actual retreat dates, actual cohort caps. State the factual constraint. Never manufacture scarcity.
- **Urgency** -- ONLY when true: enrollment actually closes on a date, a retreat is actually filling, early pricing actually expires. State the fact. Never create false deadlines.
- **Loss aversion** -- Frame as "what becomes possible when you take this step" and "what opens up for you," NOT "what you will miss" or "what you will lose." The orientation is toward possibility, not fear.

### Never Use

These tactics are prohibited regardless of effectiveness:

- Fabricated scarcity (fake "only 3 spots left" when seats are open)
- False urgency (countdown timers on evergreen offers, invented deadlines)
- Fear-based manipulation (catastrophizing the cost of inaction)
- Shame triggers (implying the reader is broken, stuck, or failing)
- "You will regret this" framing (weaponizing regret aversion)
- Countdown timers on offers that have no actual expiration
- Dark patterns in forms, checkout, or opt-in flows
- Fake social proof (invented testimonial counts, fabricated logos)

### Voice Calibration Cross-Reference

All copy using these principles must also pass the `voice-calibration` skill's
E1 rule:

> The reader is on their journey, not broken. Any psychology that positions the
> reader as broken, deficient, or incomplete to create purchase motivation
> violates the brand.

If a model application requires framing the reader as lacking something in order
to work, that application fails the ethics check and must be reframed or
discarded.

## GEO Application Note

**Generative Engine Optimization**: Mental models apply to structuring content
that AI search engines evaluate as authoritative material.

- **Authority + First Principles**: Structure pages with clear, well-sourced
  claims that AI systems can extract as definitive answers
- **Jobs to Be Done**: Frame content around the question the searcher is actually
  asking, not the answer you want to give
- **Framing + Contrast**: Present information in clear before/after or
  problem/solution structures that AI systems recognize as comprehensive coverage
- **Social Proof + Lindy Effect**: Cite established research, long-standing
  practices, and verifiable outcomes -- AI systems weight authoritative sourcing
- **AIDA + Hick's Law**: Structure content with clear hierarchy and single focal
  points per section so AI systems can extract concise, well-organized answers

When optimizing for GEO, the goal is not to manipulate AI rankings but to
produce genuinely authoritative, well-structured content that deserves to be
surfaced as a best answer.
