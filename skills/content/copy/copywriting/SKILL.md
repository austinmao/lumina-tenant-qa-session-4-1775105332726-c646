# Copywriting Skill

## Overview

Write and audit individual marketing copy assets for the organization. This
skill handles the atomic units of marketing — single emails, subject lines,
CTAs, landing page sections, and proof blocks — as opposed to full sequences
(handled by email-sequences skill) or campaign calendars (handled by
campaign-strategy skill).

All copy is written in the active brand voice: warm, grounded, intentional, personal.
Never corporate. Never pushy. Never exploitative of spiritual seeking or
vulnerability.

## Subject Line Engineering

Subject lines are the highest-leverage copywriting surface. A 1% improvement in
open rate compounds across every send. Apply this process for every subject line:

### Curiosity Gap Formula

A curiosity gap subject line withholds one piece of information the reader
genuinely wants. It creates a specific, completable desire to open.

Test: read the subject line and ask "Do I know the answer already?" If yes,
the gap is closed. Rewrite it.

Good (gap is open): "The retreat I almost didn't go to"
Bad (gap is closed): "Our upcoming retreat has limited spots"

### Specificity Over Superlatives

Specific details outperform superlatives every time.

Good: "What happened at our February retreat in Ojai"
Bad: "Our most powerful retreat experience yet"

Good: "The 3-day decision that changed everything"
Bad: "A life-changing opportunity"

### Personal and Informal Tone

Subject lines should look like they came from a person, not a marketing system.

Good: "honest question"
Good: "quick update from the operator"
Good: "I wasn't going to send this"
Bad: "Exciting Announcement from the organization!"
Bad: "Don't Miss This Limited Opportunity"

### Always Produce 5 Options

For every email, produce a minimum of 5 subject line options. Each must be
under 50 characters. Lead with transformation or tension — never the event
name. Avoid colons and em-dashes (they read as corporate in inbox preview).

- Option A: curiosity gap (emotion-led) — withhold one piece the reader wants
- Option B: specificity (outcome-led) — concrete detail beats superlative
- Option C: personal/informal (relationship-led) — looks like it came from a person
- Option D: tension/fear reframe — name a specific fear, imply resolution
- Option E: pattern interrupt — contradict a common belief or break scroll behavior

Good pattern: "[Specific fear or question] — [implicit promise]"
Bad pattern: "Join [Event Name] - [Description of what it is]"
Bad pattern: "the organization: Our Spring Retreat Awaits"

Include an A/B test recommendation: which two to test against each other, and
which to hold as fallback for the 70% send after winner is determined.

## Preview Text Engineering

Preview text is the second-highest-leverage surface after the subject line.

### Rules
- 85-100 characters (fills mobile preview pane without truncation)
- NEVER repeat or paraphrase the subject line — the preview must add new information
- Introduce a NEW tension or specific detail that makes the subject more irresistible
- Never start with: "Join us," "You're invited," "Register for," "Don't miss"
- End with an open loop — an incomplete thought that pulls the reader to open

### Self-Test
Read the subject and preview together. If the preview could be predicted from
the subject alone, rewrite it. The preview must add information the subject withheld.

### Examples
Subject: "What actually separates overwhelming from transformational"
Bad preview: "Join us for a live event on psychedelic science"
Good preview: "Most people go in without knowing the 3 safety variables that matter most"

Subject: "honest question"
Bad preview: "We have an honest question about your wellness journey"
Good preview: "Because the answer might change what you do this weekend"

## StoryBrand Hero Framing

Every piece of body copy must position the reader as the hero, not the organization.

### Opening Paragraph Rules

Never open with:
- "We are the organization..." (brand-as-hero)
- "Our upcoming retreat features..." (product-first)
- "I'm excited to announce..." (sender-first)

Always open with:
- The reader's felt problem or aspiration ("If you've been feeling
  disconnected from yourself lately...")
- A scene the reader recognizes from their own life ("It's 2am and you're
  still awake wondering if this is what you wanted")
- A question that is already in the reader's mind ("Have you ever had a
  moment where you realized you'd been saying yes to everything except what
  actually matters?")

### Guide Positioning

After the hero-opening, the organization enters as the guide — the one who understands
the problem and has a path through it. The guide never overshadows the hero.

Format: [Hero's problem] → [Guide understands] → [Guide has a path] →
[What happens if the hero takes that path] → [CTA]

## Offer Engineering (Hormozi Value Equation)

When writing any offer copy, structure the presentation in this order:
1. Dream Outcome — the specific, measurable, believable transformation
2. Likelihood of Achievement — proof that others have gotten there (SIPI format)
3. Time to Result — how fast ("in 3 days" beats "in weeks")
4. Effort and Sacrifice — how little is required ("no experience needed")
5. Price — introduced last, after value is fully established

Never lead with price. Never lead with logistics (dates, location, format)
before the outcome is established. Logistics are details; transformation is the
offer.

## SIPI Proof Method

Social proof must be specific to be believable. Apply Say It, Prove It
Immediately format for every testimonial used in copy.

Formula: [First name], [context], came in [specific before-state] and left
with [specific observable or measurable outcome].

Write and rewrite:
- BEFORE (generic, unusable): "This retreat was amazing. I learned so much.
  — Sarah"
- AFTER (SIPI format): "Sarah, a marketing director who had not taken a full
  day off in 14 months, came in barely able to sit still for 5 minutes and
  left with a daily practice she has maintained for 8 months. — Sarah K.,
  the operator TX"

Never use a generic testimonial in copy. Rewrite to SIPI format or ask the operator
for a more specific source.

## Direct Response Discipline

Every email must have:
1. One offer (even nurture emails offer something: an insight, a resource, a
   conversation)
2. CTA discipline:
   - Sequence and nurture emails: one CTA only (never two competing CTAs)
   - Broadcast event emails (webinar invites, live event promos): 3 CTAs —
     (a) above the bullet list, (b) after the bullet list, (c) P.S. text link.
     Each uses slightly different copy. All point to the same destination URL.
3. Genuine urgency only (no fabricated deadlines, no false scarcity)

Write emotionally, not professionally. Aim for the feeling of a personal note,
not a corporate newsletter. Short sentences. Short paragraphs. One idea per
paragraph. Use "I" and "you" freely.

## Landing Page Copy

For retreat, webinar, or program landing pages, produce these sections in order:

1. Headline + subheadline (3 options each)
   - Headline: outcome-first, reader-as-hero
   - Subheadline: who this is for + what changes

2. Above the fold: hero section
   - Headline, subheadline, one supporting image description (describe the
     ideal photo, do not link to stock), and primary CTA button

3. Problem / empathy section
   - 3 to 5 bullet points naming the reader's felt experience before finding
     the organization; each bullet should be recognizable to the target reader

4. Offer overview (Hormozi sequence)
   - Dream outcome → proof → ease → investment

5. What's included (features reframed as outcomes)
   - Never list features alone; always pair with the outcome the feature
     delivers ("3 days of guided ceremony" → "3 days to fully disconnect
     and reconnect to what matters")

6. Social proof section
   - 2 to 3 SIPI-format testimonials; include photo or first-name attribution

7. FAQ (objection handling)
   - 5 to 7 questions; each answer reframes the objection as a misunderstanding
     rather than dismissing it

8. Final CTA section
   - Restate dream outcome in one sentence, then CTA button

## Copy Audit Process

When auditing existing copy, evaluate each asset against all four frameworks:

Check 1 — StoryBrand (hero framing): Does the email open with the reader's
experience? Flag any opening that leads with brand, product, or sender.

Check 2 — Hormozi (value equation): Is the dream outcome named? Is proof
present and in SIPI format? Is price introduced before value is established?

Check 3 — Kennedy (direct response): Is there an offer? For sequence/nurture
emails, is there exactly one CTA? For broadcast event emails, does the 3-CTA
pattern apply correctly? Is urgency real or fabricated?

Check 4 — Brunson (sequence logic): Does this email open a loop, resolve a
loop, or do neither? Does it move the reader toward the next step in the
sequence?

Output format for audits:
For each flag: quote the original text, name the framework rule it violates,
provide a rewrite. Do not summarize — show the problem and fix inline.

## Steps

1. Identify the asset type: subject line, email body, SMS, landing page section,
   CTA, or testimonial rewrite
2. Confirm the reader segment and campaign phase (affects tone and urgency level)
3. Apply the relevant framework rules for the asset type
4. Draft 3 options for any single-line asset (subject lines, headlines, CTAs)
5. Draft 1 complete option for body copy (with annotation below)
6. For audits: flag, quote, and rewrite each issue inline
7. End with annotation block and approval gate reminder

## Output Format

For subject lines: list 3 options with framework label per option + A/B test
recommendation.

For email body copy: full draft (no placeholders) + annotation block (framework
applied, what to A/B test, compliance note).

For landing page sections: each section labeled, full copy, headline options
numbered.

For audits: original text quoted, violation named, rewrite provided — one block
per issue.

Approval gate reminder at end of every deliverable: "This copy is ready for
the operator's review. To send, route through the email-newsletter skill (email) or
sms-outreach skill (SMS) — both require the operator's explicit approval before
delivery."

## Error Handling

- If urgency copy is requested but no real deadline exists: ask the operator for the
  real deadline before drafting; never fabricate one
- If a testimonial provided for use is generic: rewrite to SIPI format and
  confirm with the operator that the rewrite is factually accurate before embedding
  it in copy
- If asked to write copy that uses pressure tactics, manufactured scarcity, or
  exploits vulnerability (fear of illness, grief, loneliness as manipulation
  tools): flag the concern, explain why it conflicts with the organization's values,
  and offer an alternative angle that achieves the same conversion goal
  through genuine value
