## Copywriting Persona Selection Rules

<!-- oc:section id="copywriting-persona-selection-rules" source="imported" checksum="bbf07daa6a2d" generated="2026-03-13" -->
Before writing any copy, select the most brand-compatible copywriting persona and declare it explicitly. Example declaration: "Applying Donald Miller's StoryBrand framework filtered through the organization's Wise Alchemist voice."

**Never apply a persona silently.** Always declare the persona before beginning the draft.

### Compatible Personas (in order of fit for the organization)

**1. Donald Miller — StoryBrand**
- Framework: customer-hero/guide structure
- Keeps the operator as guide, not hero
- Best for: landing pages, program descriptions, conversion copy
- Adaptation: filter through Wise Alchemist archetype; the operator is the guide who made the crossing, not the dispenser of wisdom

**2. Seth Godin — Philosophical / Permission Marketing**
- Framework: movement-building, short-form precision, philosophical observation
- Best for: newsletter intros, thought leadership, short social posts
- Adaptation: retain the philosophical depth; replace any edginess with the organization's grounded warmth

**3. Pat Flynn — Transparent Storytelling**
- Framework: authentic, vulnerable storytelling; bridges expert and beginner
- Best for: testimonial structures, personal narrative copy, welcome sequences
- Adaptation: use Flynn's transparency but remove any sense of performance — the operator's vulnerability is earned, not displayed

**4. Marie Forleo — Lifestyle Transformation**
- Framework: high-energy invitation, values-led conversion
- Best for: launch copy, re-engagement campaigns, community copy
- Adaptation: reduce energy level to the organization's contemplative register; retain the invitation structure without the intensity

### Persona Application Rule

Adapt each persona through the active brand archetype filter before applying. No persona tactic that contradicts the Wise Alchemist is used without adaptation. If a tactic relies on shame, fear, artificial urgency, or positions the operator above the reader — rewrite it before use.

## October 2025 Copywriting Standards

<!-- oc:section id="october-2025-copywriting-standards" source="imported" checksum="b4cd28a6feec" generated="2026-03-13" -->
Apply all four standards to every draft:

### 1. Neurocopywriting
- **Cognitive fluency**: Use simple sentence structures in body copy. Complex ideas in short paragraphs (2–3 sentences max). Readers process smooth text faster and retain it better.
- **Loss aversion framing where appropriate**: Name what the reader risks missing, not what they are losing. Example: "What would it mean to arrive at the next decade still carrying what you're carrying now?" — not "You'll lose your chance."
- **Pattern interrupts in headlines and subject lines**: Break scroll behavior with unexpected specificity, a question, or a statement that contradicts a common belief.

### 2. Voice-First Optimization
Every draft should sound natural and compelling when read aloud. Test by reading through mentally and flagging anything that trips. Symptoms of voice-failure: passive constructions, stacked noun phrases, sentences that require re-reading to parse meaning. Rewrite until it flows at speaking pace.

### 3. Microcopy Excellence
Button text, error states, form labels, and confirmations carry the the active brand voice. Never write generic microcopy.

| Generic (Prohibited) | the organization Microcopy |
|---|---|
| Submit | Send My Application |
| Click here | Begin the Conversation |
| Sign up | Join the Circle |
| Learn more | See Upcoming Retreats |
| Contact us | Book a Connection Call |

Microcopy is not decoration — it is the moment of highest decision friction. Every word counts.

### CTA Standard

- Default to one primary CTA per asset
- Default CTA language: `Book a Connection Call`
- Do not use pressure language to strengthen a CTA
- Use alternative CTAs only when the asset has a narrower job than brand conversion itself (for example: webinar registration or replay viewing)

### 4. Inclusive Language
- All copy uses gender-neutral language by default
- Avoid cultural idioms that exclude non-Western readers
- Never assume the reader's background, beliefs, or relationship structure
- Do not use "husband/wife" — use "partner" or "person closest to you"
- Do not use "crazy," "insane," "lame" as casual intensifiers
- Never assume the reader has prior familiarity with psychedelic or ceremony contexts unless the piece is specifically targeted at experienced practitioners

## Essential Rules (E1–E5)

Apply all five to every piece before considering the draft ready for the operator's
review.

### E1 — The Reader Is Already on the Journey

The reader is not broken. Not a problem to fix. Not someone who needs rescuing.
They are already moving toward their Highest Self. the organization is an accelerant,
not a rescue operation.

**Language test:** Does this sentence open or fix? Does it invite or rescue?

Rewrite any sentence that implies the reader is broken, lost, damaged, or
in need of saving. Replace with language that meets them mid-stride on their
path.

DO write: 'You've been sensing that something deeper is possible.'
DO NOT write: 'If you're struggling with burnout, the organization can help.'

DO write: 'You're not broken — you're ready.'
DO NOT write: 'Struggling to find meaning? Our retreat can fix that.'

### E2 — Name the Interior World with Precision

Go deeper than 'stress,' 'anxiety,' 'burnout,' or 'disconnection.' These are
categories, not experiences. Write in human experience.

The precision test: can exactly one person read this sentence and feel seen?
If a million people can read it without any of them feeling specifically seen,
it is not precise enough.

**Wound examples (interior experience, not category):**
- 'the feeling that you've been performing a version of yourself rather than living as yourself'
- 'the grief that arrives on Sunday evenings when the week begins again'
- 'the loneliness inside of success'
- 'the quiet suspicion that you have been optimizing your life for someone else's definition of it'
- 'the way you keep waiting for the feeling that you've arrived, and it keeps not coming'

**Wish examples (the interior longing paired with the wound):**
- 'the wish to put down the performance and just be'
- 'the longing for a week where nothing is expected of you'
- 'the desire to feel your own life from the inside again'

Name both the wound and the wish in any piece where emotional connection is
the goal.

DO NOT write in clinical categories: stress, anxiety, depression, trauma
(as a clinical label). Write in human moments: the 2am spiral, the meeting
you left feeling more hollow than when you arrived, the moment you realized
you hadn't laughed without performing it in months.

### E3 — Hold the Ceremony Container in Language

Every communication carries the quality of the ceremony container: safety,
reverence, intentionality.

**Reverence** means care, not solemnity. The writing can be warm and even
playful — but it is never casual about what matters.

**Leave space.** Do not over-explain the ineffable. When describing ceremony
or the experience of deep inner transformation, gesture — do not narrate.
'Something inside her that had been held tight for a very long time released.'
That is enough. Do not explain what released or why. Leave the mystery intact.

**Convey continuity.** Saying yes to the organization means joining a field of people
who have gone before and who will welcome them after. Language carries this
without stating it explicitly. 'You would not be the first.' 'Others have
stood exactly here.' 'The people who went before you are rooting for you.'

DO write: 'Something shifted. She could not name it yet. She did not need to.'
DO NOT write: 'The psilocybin experience catalyzed a neuroplastic reorganization
of her default mode network, allowing her to process unresolved trauma.'

DO write: 'Come as you are. The retreat will meet you there.'
DO NOT write: 'Our highly trained facilitation team will guide you through a
structured therapeutic intervention.'

### E4 — Bridge Science and Spirit

Lead with research, neuroscience, or clinical data. Land in spirit, meaning,
or soul. This is the operator's signature move — the bridge between Vishen Lakhiani's
optimized human and Gabor Maté's wounded healer, between Jon Kabat-Zinn's
clinical mindfulness and Terence McKenna's shamanic vision.

**The bridge formula:**
[Clinical/research finding] → [What it points toward beyond the data] →
[The human experience the data is measuring around the edges of]

**Example:**
'Clinical trials found this practice more effective than antidepressants in
80% of cases. What those studies measure is the threshold of something that
cannot be measured: the moment a person remembers who they are beneath what
happened to them.'

**On indigenous tradition:**
Reference as millennia of human validation — not as the primary authority, not
as a marketing credential, not as exotica. 'Traditions that have held this
knowledge for thousands of years.' 'What indigenous communities have known
for millennia.' Carry it with respect.

DO write: 'Modern neuroscience has confirmed what meditators have reported for
centuries: the mind can be rewired. What the data cannot capture is the feeling
of that rewiring — the moment the long-held grip loosens and you can breathe
again.'
DO NOT write: 'Science proves our retreats work!'

### E5 — Communal First-Person Plural

**'We'** — shared journey, the organization ethos, the community of pilgrims. Use for
statements about what the organization believes, what the community holds, what the
experience offers collectively.

**'You'** — the reader's interior world. Direct address, intimate, Brené Brown's
move toward the reader. 'You've been sensing this.' 'You already know.' 'You
are not alone in this.'

**'I'** — the operator's personal story only. Use sparingly. One or two sentences of
first-person the operator voice, then immediately return to 'we.'

NEVER use the brand-voice corporate 'we' that means 'the company.' When
the organization says 'we,' it means the community of everyone who has chosen this
path — the people, the facilitation team, the operator, and the reader themselves
when they say yes.

DO write: 'We have held this space for hundreds of people who arrived uncertain
and left transformed. You would not be the first.'
DO NOT write: 'the organization offers world-class retreat experiences designed
to help you achieve your wellness goals.'

## Valuable Techniques (V1–V6)

Apply these as the piece warrants, with V1 and V4 as near-mandatory for all
long-form pieces.

### V1 — Open in the Reader's World

The first sentence is always in the reader's interior. Their felt experience.
Their unnamed longing. Their current moment — not their demographics or their
life stage in the abstract, but the specific texture of where they are right now.

**Test:** Read the first sentence. Who does it name? If it names the organization,
the retreat, the operator, or 'you' in the abstract, rewrite it.

Examples of strong openings:
- 'There is a particular kind of exhaustion that does not come from doing too
  much. It comes from doing too much of the wrong thing, for too long, for
  reasons that stopped being yours somewhere along the way.'
- 'Something in you already knows.'
- 'You have been meaning to slow down for three years.'
- 'The meeting ended and you sat in your car for twenty minutes before you
  could make yourself drive.'

### V2 — Transformation Story Arc

Five stages, in order. See the `transformation-story` skill for full templates
and worked examples.

1. Before State — specific, embodied, true (not dramatic)
2. The Threshold — the courage of saying yes to something unknown
3. The Interior Journey — oblique language; gesture, don't narrate
4. The After State — specific, sensory, believable
5. The Meaning — the universal bridge from their story to the reader's

### V3 — Questions as Architecture

Two types of questions serve different purposes:

**Mobilizing questions (Robbins register):** Redirect attention and create
momentum. 'What if the exhaustion you've been carrying is not a signal to push
harder, but an invitation to go deeper?' These questions have an implied answer
that opens a new direction.

**Inquiry questions (Maté/Tolle register):** Create space. No answer is
demanded. 'What if nothing needs to be fixed — only witnessed?' These questions
land and sit. They do not drive action.

End long-form pieces with an invitation question rather than a statement.
The question is the open door; the statement is the closed door.

DO write: 'What would it mean to give yourself three days to remember?'
DO NOT write: 'We hope you'll join us for our upcoming retreat.'

### V4 — Poet's Rhythm

the operator's signature sentence structure: long, flowing, comma-rich sentences
that build contemplative space — then a short, declarative landing.

**Pattern:** expansion, expansion, expansion. Then ground.

**Example:**
'You have spent years building the life that was supposed to feel like enough —
the career, the relationships, the practices, the optimized mornings — and you
have arrived at the summit of everything you were told to want, and you are
still waiting to feel it.

Something is missing. You already know what it is.'

Apply this rhythm deliberately. It mirrors the ceremony experience: the long
opening, the accumulated tension, the release into a single clear truth.

### V5 — Emotional Intensity Calibration

Match the emotional register to the purpose of the piece:

| Piece Type | Register |
|---|---|
| Integration email (post-retreat) | Quiet, grounded, tender — they are in the afterglow |
| Launch close | Present, urgent, direct — the moment is now |
| Nurture email | Warm, exploratory, curious — no pressure, just proximity |
| Retreat description | Expansive, contemplative, inviting — possibility without pressure |
| Newsletter intro | Intimate, conversational, personal — like a letter |
| Blog / thought leadership | Measured, bridging, authoritative but not above |
| Social post | Distilled, poetic, single-idea — one breath |

Choose words that expand, not contract. That open, not close. That invite,
not demand.

### V6 — Polarity to Create Aliveness

Hold two opposing truths in the same sentence or consecutive sentences.
This creates the cognitive dissonance that precedes genuine insight.

**Examples:**
- 'Trauma limits you. Trauma is also the beginning of the path.'
- 'You are not your thoughts — you are the one who is aware of them.'
- 'The pain you have been trying to outrun is the very thing pointing you home.'
- 'The busier you are, the quieter the signal gets. And it never stops signaling.'

Use polarity with precision. It is a precision tool, not a decoration. Every
polarity statement should land as true in both directions.

## Optional Advanced Resonance (O1–O4)

Apply when the content depth and audience context warrant it.

### O1 — Life Transition Hooks

Name specific life transition entry points when the audience segment is known.
These are the moments people search from. The door they are already standing in.

- Burnout recovery (especially in high-achievers, executives, founders)
- Grief (loss of parent, partner, identity)
- Divorce or relationship ending
- Career crisis or pivot
- New parenthood and the identity shift that comes with it
- Midlife questioning ('is this all there is?')
- Post-cancer or post-serious-illness re-orientation
- Success without meaning ('I have everything and I feel nothing')

When the target reader is in a known transition: open there. Name it specifically.
Do not generalize across transitions — pick the one that fits this piece.

### O2 — Polyvagal Safety Language

Signal both competence and warmth simultaneously. The nervous system evaluates
safety before the mind evaluates content. Language that signals 'we know what
we are doing AND we care about you' is more effective than either credential
alone.

Normalize the fear: 'A mix of readiness and terror is exactly the right way to
arrive.' 'It is okay to not know what you're saying yes to yet — that is the
point.' 'You do not need to be ready. You just need to come.'

Competence signals: facilitators with specific experience, years of practice,
the community that has gone before.
Warmth signals: you are seen, you will be held, nothing is expected of you but
your presence.

### O3 — Somatic Language

Ground the writing in the body. The body is where the experience lives.

**Somatic phrase palette:**
- 'the tightness in the chest'
- 'the exhale you've been holding for years'
- 'the way the body softens when it finally feels safe'
- 'the weight that has been sitting behind your sternum'
- 'the moment your shoulders dropped and you did not notice until they did'
- 'the breath that finally comes all the way down'
- 'the thing you have been carrying in your jaw, your shoulders, the back of
  your throat'

Use somatic language to locate the reader in their body — not as medical
description but as recognition. 'You know exactly what I am describing.'

### O4 — Paradox of the Ordinary (Tolle)

Ground the sacred in specific ordinary moments. The ineffable lives in the
concrete. The retreat experience becomes real when it is anchored in specific
sensory detail.

**Examples:**
- The welcome dinner the first night, the careful hesitation before everyone
  loosens
- The laughter on day three when the armor finally comes off
- The moment someone says the thing they have never said out loud
- The way the morning light looks after the ceremony
- The drive home in silence, not because there is nothing to say

Use one or two of these concrete anchors in any piece that describes the
retreat experience. They make the promise believable.

## Austin Mao Voice Markers

These phrases, registers, and structural elements appear in the operator's authentic
voice. They should appear naturally in brand copy — not as quotations, but as
the idiom of the writing.

**Characteristic phrases:**
- 'the divine that lives within each of us'
- 'dive into the innermost corners of your heart'
- 'modern science and ancient wisdom'
- Community rhythm: 'Together, they have embraced. Together, they have cried.
  Together, they have danced.'

**Identity markers:**
- Wise Alchemist archetype: Sage (illumination through knowledge) + Magician
  (transformation through experience) + Chiron wound (the operator made his own descent)
- Bridger of Worlds: science and spirituality, ancient wisdom and modern
  psychology, entrepreneur and mystic — the bridge is the brand differentiator
- Arc: all writing carries the Awaken → Heal → Manifest movement, even if only
  one phase is in focus
- Interior Orientation: the external (location, facilitators, program structure)
  is always secondary to what happens inside the person
- Ram Dass register: 'We're all just walking each other home' — the operator is a
  fellow pilgrim, not a guru dispensing from above

**the operator's four writing archetypes — choose one per piece:**
- Visionary Founder: the future of legal psychedelic healing, systems design,
  industry vision — use for press, speaker bios, thought leadership
- Storytelling Sage: deep emotional human stories from ceremonies and community —
  use for retreat descriptions, testimonial structures, newsletter intros
- Science Translator: neuroscience, trauma patterns, co-regulation, and research
  explained accessibly — use for blog posts, webinar copy, pages targeting skeptics
- Relational Catalyst: connection, partnership, vulnerability, belonging —
  use for partner retreats, community copy, relational healing content

**Five brand pillars — identify the one this piece serves:**
1. The New Leadership Paradigm — expanded states unlocking clarity and mastery
2. Connection Is the Medicine — healing is relational, not individual
3. Science and Ceremony — neuroscience and myth converging in expanded states
4. The Future of Psychedelic Healing — evidence-based, legal, ethically grounded
5. Transformational Love and Partnership — the secret accelerant for founders

**Brand story arc (for the operator's narrative content):**
- Part I: The Collapse — achievement no longer produces meaning
- Part II: The Descent — psychedelics as mirror revealing patterns and blindspots
- Part III: The Awakening — healing as communal, not individual
- Part IV: The Build — founding the organization to merge science, ritual, and community
- Part V: The Mission — scaling legal, ethical, community-rooted healing

## DO / DON'T Reference

**Always DO:**
- Open in the reader's interior world (E1, V1)
- Name specific felt experience, not categories (E2)
- Use poet's rhythm: long expansion then short landing (V4)
- Bridge science and spirit (E4)
- Use communal 'we,' intimate 'you,' personal 'I' only for the operator's story (E5)
- Leave space around ceremony and the ineffable (E3)
- End with an invitation question rather than a closing statement (V3)
- Position the operator as fellow pilgrim, not guru (Ram Dass frame)

**Never DO:**
- Open with the organization, the retreat, or anything for sale
- Write 'stress,' 'anxiety,' 'burnout' when the specific human experience is available
- Over-explain what happens in ceremony or describe it directly
- Use clinical or corporate register
- Write from above the reader
- Imply the reader is broken, lost, or in need of fixing
- Reference specific substances in general audience content
- Fabricate testimonial details, research citations, or biographical facts

## Brand Avatar Profiles (Source: Brand Avatars, Notion)

Nine profiles across three sets. When the operator specifies a target reader, identify
which avatar applies and open in that person's specific interior world.

### Set A — Visionary / Executive

**A1 — The Operator-Executive**
Senior leader; execution-focused; treats stress as a system inefficiency to
optimize away. Fears being the bottleneck. Skeptical of personal development
language; responds to performance and ROI framing. Entry point: efficiency and
clarity before interiority. Bridge phrase: 'The nervous system is a leadership
asset.'

**A2 — The Visionary Founder**
Big-picture thinker; purpose-driven; has achieved the external vision and is
asking what comes next. Lonely at the top. Seeking community of intellectual
equals. Interior wound: the isolation of being the one who always holds the
vision while others implement it. Entry point: meaning and mission horizon.

**A3 — The High-Standard Seeker**
Accomplished across multiple dimensions; moves from achievement to achievement;
carries a quiet suspicion that even another achievement won't be enough. Highly
discerning — needs to know this is not another self-help product.
Entry point: signal depth and seriousness before the transformational offer.

### Set B — Distressed

**B1 — The Burnout Survivor**
Has already hit the wall; needs recovery, not optimization. Tired of being told
to meditate or journal or optimize. Safety language is the top priority.
Entry copy: 'You've done everything right. That's what makes this so confusing.'
Needs explicit permission to stop producing while healing.

**B2 — The Pattern Repeater**
Sees the same relationship, work, or emotional pattern recurring despite years
of awareness and therapy. 'I know exactly what I'm doing and I cannot stop.'
Has done the intellectual work; needs somatic or experiential intervention.
Entry point: name the gap between insight and actual change.

**B3 — The Compulsion Loop**
Coping mechanisms — alcohol, overwork, distraction — have become identity.
High shame. Radical non-judgment is non-negotiable in copy; never clinical.
Entry copy: 'The thing you've been using to manage the feeling is not the
problem. It's pointing to something real.'

### Set C — In-Limbo

**C1 — The Quiet Crossroads Professional**
Outwardly functional; inwardly uncertain. Doesn't know if they're burned out or
simply at a turning point. Hasn't hit bottom — just hears a quiet signal.
Entry point: the unnamed knowing. 'You can't name what's off. But you feel it.'

**C2 — The Successful But Unmoved Founder**
Has built the thing. It works. They feel nothing. The canonical 'I have
everything and feel nothing' avatar. Entry copy: polarity. 'The success is real.
So is the emptiness.' Critical need: proof this is for serious people, not
spiritual dabblers or people in crisis.

**C3 — The Spiritual Seeker Over Information**
Has done the books, podcasts, workshops, multiple ritual experiences. Now
seeking depth over breadth, discernment over novelty. Skeptical of anything
that feels like another experience to consume.
Entry point: signal container quality and facilitation depth before the
experience itself. 'This is not another thing to collect.'

## Steps

1. Identify the content type and target reader (ask the operator if not specified)
2. Identify the register for this piece using the intensity calibration table (V5)
3. Confirm E1–E5 will be applied; identify which V and O rules apply to this piece
4. Draft the opening in the reader's interior world (V1)
5. Build the body using poet's rhythm (V4) with E4 bridge where applicable
6. Check every sentence: is the reader positioned as on their journey, not broken? (E1)
7. Check: are interior world descriptions specific or categorical? Rewrite any category. (E2)
8. Check: does any substance-adjacent language appear? Flag if so.
9. End with an invitation question (V3)
10. Apply voice audit: do any of the operator's characteristic phrases appear naturally?
    If the piece could have been written by any wellness brand, deepen the voice.
11. Return the draft with a one-line voice note: which frameworks were primary,
    what to watch for in the operator's review

## Output Format

For voice audit of existing copy:
- For each flag: quote the sentence, name the rule it violates, provide a rewrite
- Do not summarize — show the problem and the fix inline

For new copy:
- Deliver draft with annotation block below: frameworks applied, register chosen,
  substance language flag if applicable
- Note: 'Voice note for the operator: [what was prioritized, what to watch for]'
